Course Description

This course explores interactive design. Emphasis will be placed on technical fundamentals of both User Experience (UX) and User Interface (UI) as well as concept development and the application of Graphic Design fundamentals. The primary medium for the course will be web design but the topics discussed translate to other interactive media, such as mobile apps, screen-based kiosks, and more.

Throughout each unit there will be readings, online tutorials, demos, and exercises that will provide overviews of the necessary skills/knowledge needed to succeed in this class. Critiques will serve as an opportunity for you and your peers to present your work and receive feedback for further fine tuning. The course will be a mix of instruction, usually via discussion or technical demonstrations and studio time, which is free time for you to work on your projects and receive closer attention from the instructor.

In this studio we will concentrate on learning fundamental web design technologies: HyperText Markup Lanugage (HTML), Cascading Style Sheets (CSS), and more. There are a huge number of platforms and services available to you on the web today: OtherPeoplesPixels, WordPress, Wix, Squarespace… the list is very long. HTML and CSS are foundational, so no matter what additional web technologies you choose or what platforms come and go, they will always be in the equation. We will work with those tools to allow you maximum flexibility for the future.

Course Objectives

The objectives of Interactive Design 1 are that the student:

  • Develop a basic level of proficiency for design and production using foundational web languages HTML and CSS
  • Understand web-oriented user interface and user experience design concepts
  • Develop conceptual problem-solving skills related to user interface and user experience design on the web
  • Build a vocabulary for user interface and user experience design
  • Prototype/iterate designs by producing wireframes and mockups, whether paper-based or digital, to solve key design problems prior to the production phase of projects

These objectives will be achieved by:

  • Completing projects of increasing complexity
  • In-class and homework exercises
  • Analysis and critique of one’s own work and then applying that skill to the work of others
  • Readings, lectures, and discussions.

Grading

All projects will follow the grading system below. You receive a number for a series of project requirements: idea generation, degree of progress, depth of thinking, and following directions and craftsmanship. Your final grade is an average of those requirements for each project. If a project is worth 100 points total, each of the following five aspects of the project is worth 20 points.

Idea Generation: This is the first step in the design process. I am grading you on how you participate in the idea generating activities, if you came up with the requested amount of ideas, how well they answered the problem of the project, and how they developed into the final product. Idea generation is best documented by saving and including your process work when you turn in your project files.

Depth of Thinking: I am looking at how well you thought about the problem and the solutions you were suggesting. Did you do research? Did you look at other designs that inspired you? How is that reflected in your design solutions? Did you take the information you learned in critique and use that to develop your idea even further?

Visually Compelling: This is the quality of the final design. Does your solution to the design problem meet a level of professional standards in the field of graphic design? Did you spend the time developing your idea to the best of your ability? Does your composition utilize the fundamental principles of design?

Following Directions & Craftsmanship: I am making sure that you followed all the specifications in the project brief. Is your project the correct size? Free from typos? Did you meet all the deadlines and is the final project completed on time?

Critique Participation: Here I am making sure that you participated in critiques. Critique is an essential part of design work, both for your own work and helping your colleagues improve their work. Did you comment on your colleagues’ work in a respectful, constructive way? Did you recieve feedback in a respectful, constructive way? Were you able to clearly articulate your ideas and rationalize your design choices when speaking about your work?

About Critique

Critique is a time during class where we give useful input to improve the work of our peers. Whether any of us “like” the work from our classmates is irrelevant, so try to tailor your comments and suggestions to be as objective as possible. Key questions might include:

  • “What is the work doing?”
  • “How is the work doing what it is doing?”
  • “What do we think the designer’s intention might have been?”
  • “What associations or references does the work call to mind?”

Each student is highly encouraged to practice being as specific as possible and avoid vague and subjective statements such as: “I like...” “I don’t like...” “_____ is good,” “_____ could be better,” etc.

A final note on critique: it is better to present what you have at a project critique, even if incomplete, than to skip critique and not participate. You will always learn something from critique. If you are considering skipping critique because you feel unprepared, please contact me so I can help.
